Flag-specific guidance:

Do NOT flag if the apology is brief and incidental, if it is part of a normal polite opener, or if the message quickly becomes clear, specific, and useful. Do NOT flag genuine corrections, necessary context, or messages where the softening is minor compared to the actual content.
- Over-Justifying: Flag only when the message spends conspicuously extra space defending why they deserve help or arguing their legitimacy in a way that is longer than needed. Do NOT flag concise background, relevant project context, debugging details, constraints, evidence, or information that helps the recipient answer.
- Vague Help Ask: Flag only when the request is genuinely too broad or underspecified to answer. Do NOT flag if the message includes concrete details, error text, a specific PR/task, a clear decision needed, or a bounded request.
- Hard Commit: Flag only when the speaker makes an unqualified promise or certainty about delivery/outcome, with no caveats, no dependency language, and no uncertainty. Do NOT flag statements that are conditional, tentative, dependent on approval, or simply describe a plan, intention, or next step. Words like “I’ll,” “we’ll,” or dates alone are not enough unless the message clearly promises the outcome as certain.
- Scope Creep: Flag only when extra features/requirements are being added on top of the current ask in the same message. Do NOT flag when the message is simply listing necessary conditions, constraints, tradeoffs, or review items.
- Unverified Assurance: Flag only when a claim is presented as a fact without evidence or confirmation, especially if it is the central point. Do NOT flag language that is explicitly tentative, inferential, clearly framed as an impression, or that cites a source/confirmation even briefly.

Do NOT flag normal direct operational instructions, review feedback, scheduling requests, policy/safety directives, or firm manager-style guidance that includes context, constraints, or a reasonable request.
- Public Callouts: Flag only when a person/team is called out in a blaming or corrective way in-channel. Do NOT flag neutral mentions, task assignments, routing work, or direct requests to a specific person.
- Dismissive Replies: Flag only when the message clearly shuts down discussion or dismisses the content with a terse negation/minimization. Do NOT flag firm but explainable disagreement or boundary-setting that includes guidance.

## Flag Definitions Being Tested
- Apology Hedge: Messages that lead with unnecessary apologies or self-blame before asking/saying something. Look for phrases like “sorry to bother,” “my bad,” “I’m probably missing something,” used as a preface rather than to correct a real mistake. Do not flag ordinary polite openings when the message is otherwise direct and useful.
- Over-Justifying: Asks for help but adds lots of extra explanation to defend the question (what they already tried, why they’re asking, why they’re blocked) in a way that is longer than needed. It reads like they’re proving they “deserve” help. Do not flag normal background, evidence, or troubleshooting detail that helps answer the message.
- Vague Help Ask: Requests help without a clear question or concrete details, making it hard to respond. Look for broad asks like “anyone know what’s wrong?” or “can someone help?” without error text, what they’re trying to do, or the exact decision needed.
- Hard Commit: Messages that make an unqualified promise about delivery/outcome with definitive language and no caveats—e.g., “will,” “guaranteed,” “locked,” “100%,” or exact dates—when the speaker is actually committing to a result. Do not flag tentative plans, conditional promises, or statements that depend on approval, scope, or blockers.
- Scope Creep: Messages that add extra features or requirements on top of what was discussed in the same message (often with “while we’re at it,” “also,” “quick add”) without acknowledging tradeoffs, time, or resourcing.
- Unverified Assurance: Statements that present assumptions as facts: “already approved,” “engineering said yes,” “legal is fine,” “customers will love it,” without citing a source, link, or confirmation (no “I think/maybe”).
- Abrupt Commands: Messages that are mostly directives with no greeting, softener, or collaboration language. Look for imperatives (“do X”, “ship it”), short sentences, and lack of “please/can we” or any rationale. Do not flag clear operational direction, policy enforcement, or concise manager-style instructions when they are justified by context or include constraints.
- Public Callouts: Calling out a person or team in a channel in a corrective/blaming way. Look for @mentions paired with criticism or “why didn’t you…” phrasing, especially in public channels instead of a neutral request.
- Dismissive Replies: Responses that shut down discussion without engaging the content. Look for terse negations or minimizing phrases like “no”, “not happening”, “whatever”, “obviously”, “don’t care”, with little or no explanation.
